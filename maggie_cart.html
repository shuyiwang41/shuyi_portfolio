    Setting the Scene

    OVERVIEW

    Maggie's Grocery Cart Attachment is a client-centered assistive technology project designed to support participation in grocery shopping for a wheelchair user. The design was driven by Maggie's family's request to continue using their existing grocery cart and to pursue a customizable, 3D-printed solution rather than purchasing new equipment.

    The project resulted in a low-cost, removable, 3D-printed connector that allows a wheelchair and grocery cart to be safely maneuvered as a single unit.

    CLIENT & CONTEXT

    Maggie is a 30-year-old woman with spastic quadriplegic cerebral palsy. She is non-speaking and uses a tilt-in-space wheelchair, AAC technology, and full-time caregiver support. Grocery shopping is an important instrumental activity of daily living (IADL) for Maggie, not only for functional reasons but also for social participation and autonomy.

    The challenge arose because Maggie's caregivers could not safely push both her wheelchair and a grocery cart at the same time. This physical limitation created a participation barrier, preventing Maggie from actively engaging in grocery shopping with her family or peers.

    PROBLEM DEFINITION

    The core problem was not a lack of motivation or support, but a mismatch between existing tools and Maggie's physical context. Standard grocery carts and wheelchairs are not designed to function together, and while commercial solutions exist, they often require purchasing a new cart or making permanent modifications.

    Key Constraints:

    • Must work with the family's existing grocery cart
    • Customizable through 3D printing
    • Removable (not permanent modification)
    • Low-cost and reproducible
    • Tailored to Maggie's wheelchair geometry
    PROJECT GOAL

    Enabling Participation

    Design a physical attachment that would allow a caregiver to maneuver Maggie's wheelchair and a grocery cart simultaneously as a single, integrated unit. This would enable Maggie to actively participate in grocery shopping with her mother or day program peers, reinforcing her independence and social inclusion.

    01 →

    Research & Constraints

    USER RESEARCH

    Before designing, we examined Maggie's physical setup, her wheelchair configuration, and the environments in which the device would be used.

    Key Considerations:

    • Grocery shopping occurs weekly in local stores
    • Setup and removal handled by caregivers
    • Must account for tilt-in-space wheelchair angle
    • Varying cart geometries across different stores
    • Real-world turning forces and maneuverability
    MARKET RESEARCH

    We conducted market research on existing wheelchair-cart solutions. Commercial options were either cart-only add-ons, expensive, or inflexible. This reinforced the need for a custom, user-specific solution rather than an off-the-shelf product.

    Wheelchair Shopping Cart Add-On (cart only)

    Sit & Shop (cart only)

    $515.95 from ADCO Industries

    Wheelchair Clamp & Attachment Holder

    DESIGN STRATEGY

    Given the family's request to retain their existing grocery cart and their expectation of a 3D-printed solution, we pursued a custom connector (RAM mount) rather than a commercial add-on.

    Why Custom 3D Printing?

    • Design specifically around Maggie's wheelchair geometry
    • Account for real forces during turning and maneuvering
    • Support rapid iteration and low-cost experimentation
    • Enable quick dimensional adjustments based on feedback
    02 →

    Prototyping & Iteration

    PROTOTYPE 1

    Initial Testing

    The first prototype focused on validating basic geometry and attachment feasibility.

    Key Learnings:

    • We did not have the actual cart with us to measure at this stage
    • Fit issues with square grocery cart
    • Initial angled ball joint was unnecessary
    • Challenge of using proxy wheelchairs that didn't match Maggie's chair

    Needed size adjustments

    Joint of ball does not need to be at an angle

    PROTOTYPE 2

    Refinement & Testing

    The second prototype incorporated size adjustments, removed the ball angle, and was customized in Maggie's favorite color, purple. This version was tested directly with Maggie and provided critical insight into real-world behavior.

    Critical Discovery:

    While the attachment worked well when moving straight, it popped off during turns, revealing the need for a fully enclosed design.

    DESIGN EXPERIMENTS

    Exploring Enclosure Strategies

    To address stability issues, we explored three enclosure strategies:

    1. Screws & Nuts

    Secure but difficult to manipulate

    2. Tenon-and-Mortise

    Lacked printing precision

    3. Latch Mechanism ✓

    Best balance of stability and ease of use

    REFINEMENTS

    We selected the latch mechanism and refined the joint structure to increase durability. To address the risk of losing small components, the pin was tethered to the device body, improving usability and transport.

    03 →

    Final Construction

    FABRICATION

    The final device was fabricated using PLA with 50% infill, chosen for its balance of strength and print reliability.

    Process:

    1. CAD modeling based on precise measurements
    2. Multiple print iterations at the Bray Lab
    3. Manual finishing to smooth edges and improve fit

    Total material cost: Approximately $40, making the solution accessible and reproducible.

    EVALUATION & FEEDBACK

    In final trials, the attachment performed reliably when moving straight and remained securely attached under force. However, caregivers reported that turning felt stiff due to the combined width and length of the wheelchair–cart system.

    DESIGN CONSTRAINT

    The rigid connection prioritizes stability but limits lateral flexibility, creating resistance during turns when maneuvering in tight retail aisles.

    04 →

    Outcomes & Impact

    IMPACT

    Despite limitations, the project successfully removed a major participation barrier for Maggie. The attachment enabled grocery shopping as a shared, inclusive activity rather than a task Maggie had to observe from the sidelines.

    The project also demonstrated how small, well-considered physical interventions can have outsized impact on autonomy and quality of life.

    MY ROLE & LEARNING

    As the engineering psychology student on the team, I focused on translating user needs into physical design decisions and evaluating usability tradeoffs.

    Key Takeaways:

    • Minor geometric choices can significantly affect usability, safety, and user experience
    • Interdisciplinary collaboration between engineering and occupational therapy is essential
    • Iterative prototyping with direct user feedback leads to better solutions
    • Real-world constraints often reveal design opportunities that theory alone cannot
    DELIVERABLES
    • Custom 3D-printed wheelchair-cart connector
    • CAD files for reproducibility
    • Documentation of design process and iterations
    • Usability evaluation with client and caregivers
