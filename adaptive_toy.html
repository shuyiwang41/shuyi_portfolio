    Adaptive Switch Toy

    Hardware modification enabling accessible play through external switch activation

    Context & Motivation

    BACKGROUND

    Many commercially available toys require fine motor control, precise finger movements, or sustained grip strength—capabilities that are not accessible to all children. For children with motor impairments, these design assumptions can turn play into a frustrating or fully inaccessible experience, limiting opportunities for independent exploration and engagement.

    Adaptive switch toys are a common intervention in assistive technology, allowing a single, large, low-force button to control toy activation. This project was completed as part of an Assistive Technology course, where our goal was to modify an existing toy so it could be operated using an external adaptive switch that is easier to press and more forgiving in terms of movement precision.

    01 →

    Users & Accessibility Needs

    WHO IS THIS FOR?

    This adaptation is designed for children who experience difficulty with conventional toy interfaces, including:

    • Children with cerebral palsy who have limited fine motor control or involuntary movements
    • Children with muscular dystrophy or low muscle tone, who cannot apply sustained pressure to small buttons
    • Children with spinal cord injuries or limited hand mobility
    • Children with developmental motor delays, who benefit from large, high-contrast, low-force inputs

    For these users, a large adaptive button switch allows activation using a broader range of motions, such as pressing with the palm, forearm, elbow, or even the head.

    02 →

    Design Goal

    DESIGN INTENT

    The goal of this project was to adapt an existing toy so that:

    • The toy can be activated using a large external switch
    • Minimal force is required to trigger the toy
    • The modification is durable, safe, and reversible
    • The toy remains visually and functionally intact after modification

    We selected a Dream Rotating Projection Lamp, a toy that provides visual stimulation through light and motion, making it especially engaging for children with limited mobility.

    03 →

    Technical Approach

    UNDERSTANDING THE TOY

    We began by disassembling the projection lamp to understand its internal structure, identifying:

    • The power source and control board
    • The original on/off switch
    • Contact points responsible for activating the motor and light

    By mapping the existing circuitry, we identified where an external switch could be introduced without interfering with the toy's core functionality.

    HARDWARE MODIFICATION PROCESS

    1. Opening the Toy

    The lamp casing was carefully opened using precision tools to avoid damaging the plastic shell or internal components.

    2. Identifying the Electrical Connections

    After opening the projection lamp, we needed to determine which wires and contact points controlled the toy's activation. Because the internal components were not labeled, we identified the correct connection points through direct testing.

    By briefly touching the exposed wire leads to different points on the circuit board, we observed when the light and rotation were triggered. This hands-on testing allowed us to quickly identify the two points that completed the circuit and activated the toy. Once these points were confirmed, they became the locations where the adaptive switch would later be connected.

    3. Creating an External Interface

    We drilled a small opening in the lamp's outer shell to allow the adaptive switch wire to exit cleanly. The hole placement was chosen to:

    • Avoid internal components
    • Minimize strain on the wire
    • Preserve the toy's balance and appearance

    4. Soldering the Adaptive Switch

    We soldered the adaptive switch wires directly to the control board at the same contact points used by the original activation mechanism. This allowed the external switch to function in parallel with the toy's internal circuitry.

    Key considerations included:

    • Ensuring solid solder joints to prevent intermittent activation
    • Using heat shrink tubing to insulate exposed connections
    • Securing wires internally to reduce stress and movement

    5. Reassembly & Testing

    After confirming electrical continuity and correct behavior, the lamp was reassembled. The adaptive switch was tested for:

    • Activation reliability
    • Required force
    • Responsiveness across repeated presses
    04 →

    Outcome

    FINAL RESULT

    The adapted projection lamp can now be activated using a large, accessible button switch, enabling children with limited motor control to independently control the toy's light and motion. The modification preserved the toy's original function while significantly lowering the physical and cognitive barriers to interaction.

    Reflection & Learning

    WHAT I LEARNED

    This project deepened my understanding of accessibility at the hardware level, highlighting how small physical design changes can have a large impact on user autonomy.

    Key takeaways include:

    • Translating user needs into electrical and mechanical design decisions
    • Gaining hands-on experience with soldering, circuit analysis, and enclosure modification
    • Appreciating the importance of durability and safety in assistive devices
    • Seeing how assistive technology often involves adapting existing systems, not reinventing them
