    Project Overview

    CONTEXT

    What is HumanLink?

    HumanLink is a concept-to-MVP project that makes anthropometric (human body measurement) data easier to find, filter, trust, and visualize—especially for individuals and smaller teams who don't have access to private corporate databases. HumanLink also explores a contribution model where users can upload measurements to expand representation over time.

    PAIN POINTS

    Anthropometric measurements shape everyday products (chairs, tools, wearables), but access to high-quality datasets is uneven. Large companies often build private, complex internal databases, while individuals, students, and smaller organizations rely on fragmented or outdated sources.

    From our early framing, existing databases frequently fall short on:

    • Representative data
    • Filtering by demographics
    • Regular updates
    • Clear visualization
    GOALS

    HumanLink aims to be an all-encompassing, user-friendly database where users can:

    • Find anthropometric data quickly and confidently
    • Filter data by relevant demographics/standards
    • Understand results through visualization and statistical context
    • Contribute data to improve representation over time

    Research Approach & Insights

    APPROACH

    We used a qualitative, multi-user research approach to understand how different disciplines search for and apply anthropometric information.

    • Method: One-on-one interviews (in-person + Zoom) to support follow-ups and deep context
    • Recruitment: Personal connections, cold outreach (LinkedIn), and Tufts community
    USER INTERVIEWS

    We interviewed 17 total participants across:

    • Human Factors Engineers (HFEs)
    • Biomedical Engineers (BMEs)
    • Artists
    • Students (engineering + non-engineering)
    KEY INSIGHTS

    Across disciplines, we repeatedly heard that:

    • Private databases are often too complex and may require a gatekeeper role just to retrieve data
    • People without private databases often depend on standards documents, even when those sources are not representative
    • Users strongly prefer visual, minimally text-heavy interfaces to reduce cognitive load
    • Many users are excited by AI-enhanced support
    • There is real demand for inclusive datasets and edge-case representation

    Prototyping the Concept

    APPROACH

    Originally, we planned a traditional high-fidelity prototype in Figma — but early stakeholder direction encouraged us to explore AI-assisted prototyping to generate an MVP faster.

    We chose Lovable.dev because it was intuitive to edit and compatible with our IA-first workflow.

    WHY WE USED AI

    We chose to use AI to rapidly translate a validated information architecture into a functional MVP, allowing us to test system logic, data relationships, and user flows without over-investing in visual polish. This approach kept the focus on structural clarity and downstream design impact, using AI as an accelerant for systems validation rather than a replacement for design judgment.

    How we prompted effectively:

    • Structured IA inputs
    • Scope focused on functional flows over branding

    Outcomes & Impact

    DELIVERABLES
    • 17 participant interviews
    • Multi-iteration IA
    • Wireframes aligned to accessibility and cognitive load principles
    • MVP prototype
    IMPACTS

    HumanLink produced a validated system concept and MVP that improves how designers and engineers search, interpret, and apply anthropometric data. Through 17 interviews and multiple IA iterations, we learned that barriers to data use are often structural rather than purely technical access. The final design improves legibility, supports demographic transparency, and helps users reason about when a dataset is or isn't appropriate to apply.

    FUTURE DIRECTIONS

    Going forward, the next stage of HumanLink would focus on moving from conceptual validation to technical and data oriented feasibility. This includes building a lightweight backend and dataset infrastructure to support real user accounts, version control, and metadata transparency, as well as seeding an initial repository of measurements to evaluate how the system performs with live data instead of placeholder content. We would also explore partnerships with academic labs, professional human factors communities, and design education programs to source ethically contextualized datasets and strengthen trust in the platform’s contribution model. Finally, structured usability testing, with attention to interpretability, filter behavior, and user confidence in decision making, would help refine how HumanLink communicates uncertainty and supports responsible use of anthropometric data in practice.
